Overview

Challenge-Driven Scope

Visual robot policies increasingly train in simulators, neural scenes, reconstructed digital twins, or generated worlds. The hard question is no longer whether these worlds look realistic, but whether their evidence predicts closed-loop behavior after deployment.

We focus on agile aerial autonomy as a fast, safety-critical robot-learning problem. High-speed flight exposes perception, dynamics, latency, and controller mismatch quickly, while making exhaustive real-world stress testing expensive and difficult to reproduce.

Technical Thesis

The workshop connects classical photorealistic simulation (AirSim, FlightGoggles, Flightmare), domain randomization and adaptation, and newer NeRF/3D Gaussian Splatting pipelines such as NeRF2Real, COV-NeRF, SplatSim, RoboGSim, VR-Robo, RE3SIM, SOUS VIDE, Splat-Nav, GRaD-Nav++, HAMMER, and VISTA.

Real-To-Sim Worlds

Reconstruct, generate, and edit scenes with task-relevant geometry, semantics, lighting, and sensor effects.

Closed-Loop Transfer

Measure which visual, dynamics, latency, and control mismatches change policy behavior.

Deployment Evidence

Connect simulation, hardware-in-the-loop, mixed reality, and real trials through comparable logs and failures.

Challenge focus

Research Questions

Specific open problems, not a survey-style mini-conference.

01

Which Edits Matter?

Find scene, object, lighting, texture, weather, and trajectory edits that expose real policy failures.

02

What Fidelity Predicts Transfer?

Separate visual realism from closed-loop usefulness across sensors, dynamics, latency, and control.

03

How Do We Stress-Test Safely?

Use simulation and mixed reality to discover failure boundaries before damaging hardware.

04

What Should Be Reported?

Standardize perturbation suites, sim/real correlations, negative results, logs, and deployment claims.

Contributed papers

Call for Contributions

We will solicit and accept contributed workshop papers.

We will invite 2 to 4 page extended abstracts, in-progress papers, datasets, benchmark proposals, system descriptions, failure analyses, sim/real correlation studies, and position pieces from aerial and non-aerial visual robotics.

  • Each submission receives at least two reviews.
  • Criteria: relevance, clarity, technical soundness, discussion value, and artifact potential.
  • Accepted work appears as posters; selected papers receive short spotlights.
  • Main CoRL 2026 papers will not be accepted as workshop papers.
