Overview

Workshop Scope

Vision-conditioned robot policies are increasingly trained in simulation, neural scenes, reconstructed digital twins, or generated worlds before deployment on real hardware. The open question is no longer only whether a simulation looks realistic; it is whether evidence from simulation, hardware-in-the-loop, mixed reality, and real trials predicts closed-loop behavior.

Aerial robots make this gap impossible to ignore. Fast flight compresses perception, control, and safety failures into seconds, while crashes make exhaustive real-world stress testing costly and hard to reproduce.

The workshop uses aerial autonomy as a demanding testbed for a broader robot learning challenge: how should we reconstruct, edit, perturb, evaluate, log, and release visual sim-to-real evidence so claims are comparable across labs?

From photorealistic simulators to editable neural-scene testbeds

Technical Context

AirSim, FlightGoggles, Flightmare, and related simulators established visually rich aerial and autonomous-vehicle simulation. Domain randomization and adaptation then showed that transfer depends on more than appearance: camera pose, illumination, motion blur, sensor artifacts, latency, actuator dynamics, system identification, and scenario distributions can all change the policy's closed-loop behavior.

Newer NeRF and 3D Gaussian Splatting systems are shifting the field from generic simulation to real-to-sim-to-real pipelines. Recent work such as NeRF2Real, COV-NeRF, SplatSim, RoboGSim, VR-Robo, RE3SIM, SOUS VIDE, Splat-Nav, GRaD-Nav++, HAMMER, and VISTA points toward reconstructed, composable, semantic, or language-queryable worlds for policy training and evaluation.

The missing piece is community agreement on the loop: which world edits matter, which sim/real correlations should be reported, how to combine simulation with hardware-in-the-loop and mixed reality, and what negative results are necessary before deployment.

Challenge focus

Research Questions

Designed around specific open problems rather than survey talks.

01

Editable Worlds That Matter

Reconstructed, generated, or hand-built environments should expose policy-relevant failures rather than only cosmetic visual variation.

02

Fidelity Versus Usefulness

The workshop will ask which visual, dynamics, sensing, latency, and control mismatches actually change closed-loop behavior.

03

In-the-Loop Evaluation

Simulation, hardware-in-the-loop, mixed reality, and real deployment should form a staged evidence pipeline.

04

Failure-Boundary Discovery

Policies need safe stress tests that find brittle initial conditions, perturbations, and scenario parameters before hardware damage occurs.

05

Reproducible Evidence

Logs, perturbation suites, negative results, and artifacts should make visual sim-to-real claims inspectable.

Contributed papers

Call for Contributions

We will solicit and accept contributed workshop papers.

We plan to solicit 2 to 4 page extended abstracts, in-progress research papers, datasets, benchmark proposals, system descriptions, failure analyses, sim/real correlation studies, and position pieces. Submissions from aerial and non-aerial visual robotics are welcome if they address the visual sim-to-real loop.

  • Each submission will receive at least two reviews.
  • Review criteria: relevance, clarity, technical soundness, discussion value, and artifact potential.
  • Accepted contributions will be invited for posters, selected spotlights, and inclusion in the post-workshop artifact.
  • Main CoRL 2026 papers will not be accepted as workshop papers, but related work may support discussion of an open challenge.
